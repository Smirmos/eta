# Training Intensity

> Source: Friel 5th ed., Chapter 4, pp. 80–96
> Extracted: 2026-05-05

## Summary

Chapter 4 establishes the four intensity-measurement methods (RPE, pace, heart rate, power), defines functional thresholds (FTHR, FTPo, FTPa) and lactate thresholds (LT1, LT2), provides the master 7-zone table (Table 4.1) that maps RPE → zone → % of threshold for each discipline, and prescribes field-test protocols (1,000 m swim TT; 20-minute bike and run TT) plus an optional lactate-test alternative. It introduces but does not resolve intensity distribution (polarized vs. pyramidal), deferring distribution rules to Chapter 8. (pp. 81, 96)

## Measuring intensity — methods

Friel: "Currently, there are four ways a triathlete can measure workout intensity, depending on the sport: rating of perceived exertion, pace, heart rate, and power." (p. 82)

### Rating of Perceived Exertion (RPE)

- **What it measures:** the athlete's subjective opinion of effort on a 0–10 scale; "0 means no exercise intensity at all, and 1 indicates you're moving really slowly at a low level of effort. At the high end, 10 means as hard as you can go given the duration." (pp. 82–83)
- **Per-discipline applicability:** swim, bike, run — all three. "The most basic way to measure intensity, and one that all triathletes need to master." (p. 82)
- **Pros (per Friel):** universally available; no equipment; trainable skill — "becoming skilled in the use of this scale is an art that is learned by using it frequently." (p. 82)
- **Cons (per Friel):** "RPE is highly subjective. In fact, that's all it is — your opinion of how you feel while exercising." (p. 83) Athletes systematically bias rating down when training with partners: "It's common for athletes to assign a lower RPE to any given level of intensity when they are with others." (p. 83)
- **Recommended primary method:** Not Friel's primary anchor. He requires coached athletes to also have HR monitor, GPS, and bike power meter "to be more precise … you need to use tools for measuring intensity that are objective." (p. 83)

### Pace

- **What it measures:** speed expressed as time per mile or kilometer (or per 100 m/yd in swimming). (p. 83)
- **Per-discipline applicability:**
  - **Swim:** "Today, pace is still commonly used in swimming … because swimming in a pool means the distance of the workout is well controlled and there is typically a clock on the wall, pace is easily measured and therefore still the best way to convey real-time intensity." (p. 84)
  - **Run:** Less reliable than for swim because "there are seldom mile markers on the roads where running takes place, and terrain can vary considerably." GPS has improved this. (p. 84)
  - **Bike:** "That didn't work so well for cyclists because outside factors such as wind, hills, and drafting made pace (or speed) quite imprecise." (pp. 83–84)
- **Pros (per Friel):** in pool, the most precise real-time measure (p. 84); for running, "both pace (or speed) and heart rate are valuable information to have when training." (p. 84)
- **Cons (per Friel):** unreliable for cycling due to wind/hills/drafting (pp. 83–84); imprecise for road running without GPS (p. 84).
- **Recommended primary method:** **Swim — yes** (Friel uses pace as the primary swim metric, anchored by T-time from the 1,000 m TT, p. 87). **Run — pace is one of two valid metrics alongside HR** ("I'll eventually teach you how to combine these to train your aerobic endurance fitness," p. 84). **Bike — no** (use power).

### Heart Rate

- **What it measures:** "Heart rate tells you the effort at which you are working. It does not tell you anything about performance." (p. 84) "Heart rate doesn't tell you how fast you are going; what it tells you is how much of your effort is going into the workout. When it comes to precisely measuring effort, it's the best tool we have. In that regard, heart rate is very much like RPE, only more objective." (pp. 84–85)
- **Per-discipline applicability:**
  - **Swim:** "Triathletes were among the first to use heart rate monitors but found that they didn't work very well in the early days of the sport for swimming." (p. 84) Friel uses HR for swim only as a cross-check during the T-time test ("If you wear a heart rate monitor for the swim test, determine your average heart rate for the test and subtract 5 percent to estimate your swim functional threshold heart rate for swimming," p. 88), not as the primary swim metric.
  - **Bike:** valid; FTHR set via 20-minute field test (p. 88).
  - **Run:** valid and primary historical metric — "heart rate has become the common intensity metric for runners." (p. 84)
- **Pros (per Friel):** objective measure of effort; "the best tool we have" for measuring effort precisely (p. 85); for advanced athletes, "heart rate zones change very little, if at all, throughout the season" (p. 95) so zones stay stable.
- **Cons (per Friel):** measures effort, not performance — "There are no podium positions for high heart rates." (p. 84) Limited utility for swimming (p. 84). Lag in response to changes in intensity is implicit but not stated verbatim in this chapter.
- **Recommended primary method:** **Run — yes**, alongside pace (p. 84). **Bike — co-primary with power** (Friel uses both). **Swim — no, secondary** (p. 88).

### Power

- **What it measures:** "A bike power meter measures two things: the force that's applied to the pedal and the speed at which the pedals are turned. Pedal force is called *torque*. Pedal speed is called *cadence*. The combination of the two is expressed in *watts*." (p. 85)
- **Per-discipline applicability:**
  - **Bike:** primary metric. "Since riders' acceptance of the power meter, cycling has moved from being the least to the most scientific of the three sports. The power meter has revolutionized bike training." (p. 85)
  - **Run:** "Power meters for running have been available for several years but as of this writing have not caught on, much as the bike power meter was also slow to become widely accepted in sport." (p. 85) Not used in this chapter's run zone-setting.
  - **Swim:** not addressed.
- **Pros (per Friel):** most scientific intensity metric for cycling; tells real performance (force × cadence), not just effort (p. 85); "learning how to use a power meter has the potential to improve your training and racing greatly." (p. 85)
- **Cons (per Friel):** out of scope for detail in this book — "It's beyond the scope of this book to go into the details of power meter use. To learn more about how power meters work and how to train with them, see my book *The Power Meter Handbook*." (p. 85) Power zones change with fitness, requiring retesting "about every six weeks or so" if zones are derived from lactate testing (p. 95).
- **Recommended primary method:** **Bike — yes** (primary).

## Threshold concepts

### Functional Threshold Heart Rate (FTHR)

- **Definition:** The HR anchor for the 7-zone system. Friel uses "functional threshold heart rates (FTHR)" (p. 87). He explicitly avoids age-based max-HR formulas: "don't use a formula. Do a field test to find your FTHR." (p. 89)
- **How it's measured:**
  - **Bike & run:** 20-minute field test (warmup → 20 min "high but somewhat conservative" all-out → cooldown). "Find your average heart rate for the 20-minute test. Subtract 5 percent and you have an estimate of your bike or run FTHR." (p. 91; full protocol pp. 89–91; references "T1: Functional Threshold Test" in Appendix C for bike and Appendix D for run)
  - **Swim:** "If you wear a heart rate monitor for the swim test, determine your average heart rate for the test and subtract 5 percent to estimate your swim functional threshold heart rate for swimming." (p. 88) Test is the 1,000 m TT.
- **What it anchors:** FTHR % column in Table 4.1 — i.e., HR zones for bike, run, and (optionally) swim. (p. 86)

### Lactate Threshold (LT1, LT2) and Lactate Threshold Heart Rate (LTHR)

- **Definitions (verbatim, p. 93–94):**
  - **LT1 (lactate threshold 1, "aerobic threshold"):** "When the intensity of exercise is very low (RPE 1–3), the lactate level is below 2.0 mmol/L. That level is considered the 'aerobic threshold,' which is also called 'lactate threshold 1,' or LT1. This level of accumulated lactate is found at the upper end of zone 2 for heart rate, power, and pace." (p. 93)
  - **LT2 (lactate threshold 2):** "When it reaches the level of 4 mmol/L, your breathing becomes labored and your perception of the exercise becomes increasingly 'Hard.' That's the start of your zone 4. This stage of lactate production is called 'lactate threshold 2' or LT2." (p. 94) "LT2 is often referred to as the 'anaerobic threshold,' which is misleading, since anaerobic means 'without oxygen,' which is certainly not the case. We'll just refer to it here as LT2." (p. 94)
- **How LTHR is measured:** Lab "step test" with bike ergometer or treadmill (or track with experienced technician); pool for swim. "After warming up, the athlete holds a certain easy intensity for a few minutes. At the end of that 'step' a blood sample is taken from a finger or earlobe. Then the intensity is increased slightly for a few minutes, with a blood sample taken at the end before the next step is started. This goes on until the athlete reaches a maximum level of intensity and has to stop. The technician then examines the data and identifies LT1 and LT2." (p. 94) Cost: $100–$350 per test (p. 95).
- **What it anchors:** the "Lactate Threshold Heart Rate (LTHR)" column of Table 4.1. (p. 94) "If you use the lactate test to determine your heart rate zones, you may not need a follow-up test for a few months, since heart rate doesn't change much, if at all, over the course of a season." (p. 95)
- [FLAG: The chapter uses both "FTHR" (field-test estimate of LT2-area heart rate) and "LTHR" (lab-derived). Table 4.1 shows them as separate columns. Friel's recommended workflow: lactate-test the bike and run for HR zones; field-test for power and pace zones (p. 95).]

### Functional Threshold Power — FTPo (bike)

- **Definition:** the bike power anchor. (p. 86; column in Table 4.1)
- **How it's measured:** same 20-minute test as FTHR. "All you do is subtract 5 percent from your average power (not 'normalized' power) and you have a good estimate of your FTPo." (p. 90)
- **What it anchors:** Bike Functional Threshold Power (FTPo) column of Table 4.1 — bike power zones. (p. 86)
- **Note:** "Power and pace do change, and quite a bit with focused training. If you use the lactate test to set power zones on the bike, swim, or run pace zones, you will need to retest about every six weeks or so." (p. 95)

### Functional Threshold Pace — FTPa (run)

- **Definition:** the run pace anchor. (p. 86; column in Table 4.1)
- **How it's measured:** derived from the 20-minute run test. "From the 20-minute test data, find your average mile (or kilometer) pace from your downloaded GPS. Then compute your pace zones by using Table 4.1, FTPa column. This is best done by converting average time to minutes and tenths of a minute. For example, 7 minutes, 30 seconds would be 7.5 minutes. *Add* (notice that you don't *subtract* when using pace) 5 percent to determine your FTPa." (p. 91)
- **Worked example (verbatim, p. 91):** "if your average pace for the 20-minute run test was 7.5 minutes, multiply 7.5 by 0.05 to get 0.375. Adding 0.375 to 7.5 produces your FTPa. In the example, it would be 7.875 minutes (7 minutes, 52 seconds)."
- **What it anchors:** Run Functional Threshold Pace (FTPa) column of Table 4.1 — run pace zones. (p. 86)

### T-time (swim threshold pace)

- **Definition:** "For pace, what you want to find in this time trial is called your *T-time*, which is your average pace for 100 meters (or yards)." (p. 87)
- **How it's measured:** "After warming up, swim 1,000 meters (or yards) as if it were a race." (p. 87) Then "find your 1,000-meter (or yard) finishing time in the left-hand column [of Table 4.2]. Read across the table to the right to find your swim pace training zone T-times." (pp. 87–88) Detailed protocol: "T2: Functional Threshold Pace Test" at end of Appendix B (p. 88).
- **What it anchors:** Table 4.2 swim pace zones (per 100 m/yd). (p. 92) Swim FTHR is also derived from this same test (avg HR – 5%) for the LTHR column of Table 4.1. (p. 88)

## Zone derivation formulas

### From the 20-minute bike or run field test (p. 91)

- **Inputs:** average HR over the 20-minute time trial (HR_avg), average power over the 20-minute TT for bike (P_avg), average mile/km pace over the 20-minute TT for run (Pace_avg in minutes-decimal).
- **Outputs:**
  - `FTHR = HR_avg × 0.95` — i.e., subtract 5%. (p. 91)
  - `FTPo = P_avg × 0.95` — subtract 5% from average (not normalized) power. (p. 90)
  - `FTPa = Pace_avg × 1.05` — **add** 5% (because faster pace = lower number). (p. 91)
- **Then:** apply the % bands from Table 4.1 to FTHR / FTPo / FTPa to get zone bounds.

**Worked example for FTPa (verbatim, p. 91):**
> "if your average pace for the 20-minute run test was 7.5 minutes, multiply 7.5 by 0.05 to get 0.375. Adding 0.375 to 7.5 produces your FTPa. In the example, it would be 7.875 minutes (7 minutes, 52 seconds)."

### From the 1,000 m swim TT (p. 87–88)

- **Inputs:** finishing time for 1,000 m (or 1,000 yd) all-out swim; optionally average HR during the TT.
- **Outputs:**
  - **Pace zones:** look up the 1,000 m time row in Table 4.2 → read across to find each zone's per-100 m/yd target band. (p. 88)
  - **T-time:** average pace per 100 m/yd from the test. (p. 87)
  - **Swim FTHR (optional):** `swim FTHR = HR_avg_during_swim_test × 0.95` (subtract 5%). Use Table 4.1 FTHR column to set swim HR zones. (p. 88)
- Detailed test protocol referenced as workout "T2: Functional Threshold Pace Test" at end of Appendix B. (p. 88) `[SEE: Appendix B, T2]`

### From a lab lactate (step) test (pp. 93–95)

- **Inputs:** identified LT1 and LT2 from a lab step test (typically bike ergometer or treadmill). HR / power / pace at each step is recorded.
- **Outputs:** apply the LTHR column of Table 4.1 (using LT1 and LT2 as anchors) to set HR zones; pace and power zones still derived from FTPa/FTPo via field tests, per Friel's recommendation.
- **Friel's prescription (verbatim, p. 95):** "I'd suggest doing a lactate test to set your heart rate zones for the bike and run, and field tests to set power and pace zones for all three sports."
- **Retest cadence (verbatim, p. 95):** "If you use the lactate test to determine your heart rate zones, you may not need a follow-up test for a few months, since heart rate doesn't change much, if at all, over the course of a season. Power and pace do change, and quite a bit with focused training. If you use the lactate test to set power zones on the bike, swim, or run pace zones, you will need to retest about every six weeks or so."

### Test execution rules (verbatim, pp. 89–91)

- **Field-test course requirements (run):** "a track works well because it's flat and safe." (p. 89)
- **Field-test course requirements (bike):** "a stretch of road with a wide bike lane, light traffic, no stop signs, few intersections or corners, and flat to slightly uphill (grade of less than 3 percent). You will probably need 5 to 10 miles like this depending on how fast you are." (p. 89)
- **Pre-test rest:** "the test should be done on a day when you are rested. Treat it much as you would a race by backing off your training for two or three days prior. Do short workouts only." (p. 89)
- **Warm-up:** "about 20 minutes of building intensity from a very low and steady RPE to progressively longer accelerations done at increasingly higher efforts that take you to RPE 6 (Table 4.1) for a few seconds at a time. After the last such high effort, recover for about 2 minutes at a very low RPE before starting the 20-minute test." (p. 89)
- **Pacing:** "Begin the test at a high but somewhat conservative effort. In other words, you should feel as if you could go much faster. The most common mistake that athletes make when doing this test is starting out too fast because it feels so easy for a few minutes. … The first 5 minutes should feel relatively easy. After every 5 minutes, decide whether you should go somewhat faster or more slowly for the next 5 minutes. These 5-minute changes in RPE should be slight." (p. 90)
- **Cooldown:** "At the end of the 20-minute test, begin an easy cooldown, allowing your heart rate and breathing to return to resting levels." (p. 90)
- **Hard rule:** "Do a field test to find your FTHR." — explicit rejection of age-based formulas (e.g., 220 − age), with stated max error "perhaps by as much as 20 bpm — either high or low. That's a 40-bpm range!" (pp. 88–89)

### Zone agreement across metrics (p. 95–96)

> "When you're in heart rate zone 2, you aren't necessarily in power zone 2 when cycling or pace zone 2 when running. This is not an issue about your heart, legs, or devices. It's just the way it is. … For advanced athletes, heart rate zones change very little, if at all, throughout the season. On the other hand, power zones and pace zones change significantly. As you get into better shape, your FTPa gets faster and FTPo rises. … This means that your power and pace zones also change. … all the while, heart rate zones remain fairly constant, assuming the initial testing was accurate." (pp. 95–96)

> "There is currently a great deal of debate among sports scientists on how your training time should be distributed over a season relative to your zones in Table 4.1. There are those who make the case that you should train mostly easy in zones 1 and 2, occasionally hard in zones 4 and 5, and very little in zone 3 in the middle. This is called *polarized training*. Others suggest that your training should include a significant amount of time in zone 3 with slightly less in zones 4 and 5. This is known as *pyramidal training*. ## Open questions / flags

- **[RESOLVED — zone 5b cells]** Bike FTPo, Run FTPa, "Commonly Called", and "Workout Type" cells for zone 5b (RPE 9) in Table 4.1 are merged with zone 5a in the source. Confirmed via auditor against clean PDF (see `01-zones.audit.md`). Zone 5b shares 105–120% FTPo / 90–100% FTPa / Aerobic capacity (AC) / Short intervals/equal recoveries (VHIT) with 5a; only the FTHR column distinguishes the two. (p. 86)
- **[FLAG FTHR gap]** Table 4.1 leaves 70–77% of FTHR unzoned: zone 1 ends at "less than 70%" and zone 2 starts at "78–86%". Friel does not explain. Do not invent a zone. (p. 86)
- **[FLAG LTHR phrasing]** "Heart rate at LT1 minus 10 BPM" (zone 2) and "Heart rate at LT2 plus 10 BPM" (zone 4) are ambiguous as written — they describe a boundary, not a band. Most plausible reading: zone 2 spans (LT1 − 10) → LT1; zone 4 spans LT2 → (LT2 + 10). Verify against Chapter 8 / Appendix C/D before using numeric bounds. (p. 86)
- **[FLAG FTHR vs. LTHR overlap]** Table 4.1 has both an FTHR column (% of field-test FTHR) and an LTHR column (lab-derived). They are not the same value, and Friel's recommended workflow uses lactate-test LTHR for HR zones but field-test FTPo/FTPa for power/pace zones (p. 95). Plan generator should treat FTHR and LTHR as distinct anchors and apply whichever the athlete actually has data for.
- **[FLAG primary metrics]** Friel does not name a single primary metric for bike or run; he prescribes simultaneous HR + power on the bike, HR + pace on the run, all derived from the same 20-minute test. (p. 90)
