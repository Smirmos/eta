## Summary

Chapter 7 establishes Friel's "classic / linear periodization" model and walks through the **six-step Annual Training Plan (ATP) construction process**. Output of the process: a paper or electronic plan listing weeks, dates, races, race priorities (A/B/C), assigned period (Prep / Base 1–3 / Build 1–2 / Peak / Race / Transition), and weekly volume in hours or TSS. Phase durations, recovery cadence, annual-volume guide (Table 7.2), per-subperiod weekly-volume distributions (Tables 7.5 hours, 7.6 TSS), and a fully-worked half-Ironman ATP example (Figure 7.5) are all anchored here. ## Periodization principles

### What periodization is

> "Periodization is simply a way to plan the season based on well-defined training periods over days, weeks, months, and even years. Each period has a purpose, I learned, and if the periods are structured in certain ways, they can boost fitness in time for a few select races." (pp. 159–160)

Friel uses the term **classic / linear periodization** in this chapter. (p. 161) Block periodization is mentioned and contrasted but not used here; it's covered in Chapter 9. (pp. 160–161)

### Foundational beliefs

- **Long, gradual building process.** "The starting place for your planning is adopting the belief that training must be a long and gradual building process. Peak race performance doesn't happen suddenly or mysteriously. Your body must undergo a lot of changes to become race-ready. Each of these physiological changes takes time. They cannot be rushed. You can't force your body to become fit on some sort of artificial schedule. It must be gently coaxed to a higher level of fitness by allowing it to progress naturally." (pp. 161–162)
- **The plan must be dynamic.** "To honor the body's natural progression, you *must* be willing to make changes to your plan from time to time. A rigid plan that doesn't allow for days when you are overly tired and doesn't consider the many other lifestyle demands on your time is worse than no plan at all. Your plan needs to be dynamic. Every day, you have to take into account how you are feeling. Planning flexibility is critical to your racing success. You must be willing to make changes. That almost always means resting when rest is needed." (p. 162) `[SEE: Chapter 11 — recovery on demand]`
- **Training progression: workouts become race-like.** "As the season moves forward, your workouts must become increasingly like the race. In other words, as you get closer to the day of your most important race, your workouts must gradually take on the characteristics of that race based on your goals for the race." (p. 162) Examples: race-pace runs off the bike, hills if course is hilly, open-water swims if race is open-water, heat training if race-day expected hot. (pp. 162–163)

### General → specific (base ↔ build) framing

> "Base period is for general preparation. Build period is for specific preparation." (p. 165, sidebar callout)

> "Besides calling it the base period, when the basic abilities are emphasized, this time of the year is sometimes referred to as the 'general' preparation period. The general preparation title is actually more descriptive of the purpose of the base period — to prepare in a *general* way for the latter part of the season. Essentially, when in the general preparation (base) period, you are not really training to race — you are training to train. You're preparing for the build period. The build period is often called the specific preparation period. Again, specific preparation is a good name because it implies that the emphasis of training is on workouts that are specific, or similar, to the race for which you are training." (pp. 164–165)

### Volume → Duration → Intensity progression (the "cake" analogy)

> "There's an important training progression we follow in this seasonal overview. The starting place in the base-general training period is volume. … This is a focus of the base (general) period. … Once your volume is developed to the desired level, then the focus shifts to duration — how long the workouts are. There may be fewer of them, but they are getting longer. This is still the base (general) period. They are still mostly slow and easy (zones 1 and 2). Once duration is where you want it and you have adapted to longer swims, rides, and runs, the training emphasis becomes race-like intensity instead of primarily volume and duration. This is the key marker of the build (specific) training period. Let's use an analogy to simplify the concept. In the base period you 'baked a cake.' It was a long and demanding process. Now, in the build period, it's time to 'put the icing on the cake.' This isn't easy, but it also doesn't take as much time. Once the icing is on the cake, you are ready to race." (p. 166)

### The ability triangle (Figure 7.1, p. 164)

```
           /                \

```

| Position | Ability | Category |
|---|---|---|
| Top vertex | Aerobic Endurance | Basic |
| Bottom-left vertex | Muscular Force | Basic |
| Bottom-right vertex | Speed Skills | Basic |
| Left side | Muscular Endurance | Advanced |
| Right side | Aerobic Capacity | Advanced |
| Bottom side (between MF and SS) | Sprint Power | Advanced |

Caption: "FIGURE 7.1 The basic and advanced abilities" (p. 164)

> "The basic abilities are at the corners of the ability triangle — aerobic endurance, muscular force, and speed skills. At the sides are the advanced abilities — muscular endurance, aerobic capacity, and sprint power. The critical message from Chapter 6 is that the advanced abilities are the product of the basic abilities. If you want to have good muscular endurance for your race, then you must first fully develop your aerobic endurance and muscular force. In the same way, strong anaerobic endurance results from well-developed aerobic endurance and speed skills. (As mentioned in Chapter 6, triathletes need not train for sprint power.)" (pp. 163–164)

> "In linear periodization we begin the season by focusing on the basic abilities, and as training progresses, we gradually shift the emphasis to the advanced abilities. It's not a sudden shift from basic to advanced, but a slow and gradual change involving a considerable overlap of the two categories." (pp. 164–165)

### Per-phase summary (synthesized from Tables 7.1, 7.4 and body text)

For each phase, the chapter establishes:

#### Prep
- **Purpose:** "Preparing to train" / "Preparation for training: cross-training, weights, functional strength, general athleticism." (pp. 165, 184)
- **Typical duration:** 1–4 weeks (Table 7.1, p. 165; Table 7.4, p. 184). "This can vary from only one week to as many as four weeks, or perhaps even longer if you have a big gap until it's time to start training for the new season. I should add that there have been times when I've completely omitted it for some athletes when they had a limited amount of training weeks before the first race. I've also extended it beyond four weeks when athletes had excessive time until an A-priority event and we decided to hold off on base training for whatever reason." (pp. 186)
- **Volume target (per Table 7.5, p. 190):** flat through the period — the entire prep period uses one weekly-hours value. Examples for representative annual-hours bands: **300 h/yr → 5.0 h/wk; 500 h/yr → 8.5 h/wk; 800 h/yr → 13.5 h/wk; 1,200 h/yr → 20.0 h/wk.** Full per-volume mapping in the "Tables 7.5 and 7.6" subsection below.
- **Intensity emphasis:** Basic abilities. (Table 7.1, p. 165)
- **Key workout types:** "cross-training, weights, functional strength, general athleticism." (Table 7.4, p. 184) `[SEE: Appendix B/C/D/E for sport-specific workouts; Chapter 13 for strength.]`
- **Recovery week pattern:** "Recovery not needed." (Table 7.4, p. 184)

#### Base 1
- **Purpose:** "General training: aerobic endurance, muscular force, speed skills" (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Typical duration:** 4 weeks (under 50) / 3 weeks (over 50). (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Volume target (per Table 7.5, p. 190):** progressive build wks 1–3, recovery wk 4. Examples at 500 h/yr: **wk 1 = 10.0; wk 2 = 12.0; wk 3 = 13.5; wk 4 (recovery) = 7.0.** At 800 h/yr: **15.5 / 19.0 / 21.5 / 11.5.** At 1,200 h/yr: **23.5 / 28.5 / 32.0 / 17.0.** Full per-volume mapping in the "Tables 7.5 and 7.6" subsection below.
- **Intensity emphasis:** Basic abilities; "mostly slow and easy (zones 1 and 2)" (general principle, p. 166).
- **Key workout types:** AE, MF, SS workouts. `[SEE: Appendices B/C/D — AE1, AE2, MF1–3, SS1–2]`
- **Recovery week pattern:** "Last 4–7 days of base 1." (Table 7.4, p. 184)

#### Base 2
- **Purpose:** "General training: aerobic endurance, muscular force, speed skills, limited muscular endurance" (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Typical duration:** 4 weeks (under 50) / 3 weeks (over 50). (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Volume target (per Table 7.5, p. 190):** progressive build wks 1–3, recovery wk 4. Examples at 500 h/yr: **wk 1 = 10.5; wk 2 = 12.5; wk 3 = 14.0; wk 4 (recovery) = 7.0.** At 800 h/yr: **17.0 / 20.0 / 22.5 / 11.5.** At 1,200 h/yr: **25.0 / 30.0 / 33.5 / 17.0.**
- **Intensity emphasis:** Basic abilities + limited ME. (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Key workout types:** AE, MF, SS workouts + limited ME. `[SEE: Appendices B/C/D]`
- **Recovery week pattern:** "Last 4–7 days of base 2." (Table 7.4, p. 184)

#### Base 3
- **Purpose:** "General training: aerobic endurance, muscular force, speed skills, more muscular endurance" (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Typical duration:** 4 weeks (under 50) / 3 weeks (over 50, **repeat base 3**). (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Volume target (per Table 7.5, p. 190):** progressive build wks 1–3 (the highest single-week volume of the season occurs in Base 3 wk 3), recovery wk 4. Examples at 500 h/yr: **wk 1 = 11.0; wk 2 = 13.5; wk 3 = 15.0; wk 4 (recovery) = 7.0.** At 800 h/yr: **18.0 / 21.5 / 23.5 / 11.5.** At 1,200 h/yr: **27.0 / 32.0 / 35.0 / 17.0.**
- **Intensity emphasis:** Basic + more ME. (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Key workout types:** AE, MF, SS + more ME work. `[SEE: Appendices B/C/D]`
- **Recovery week pattern:** "Last 4–7 days of base 3." (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Notes:** Friel mentions training elite Ironman triathletes through aerobic capacity work in base 3 to boost VO₂max before build, contrary to traditional linear periodization which keeps AC out of base. (p. 182) For novice triathletes (first year): "base 3 is repeated and replaces all the build subperiods." (p. 187) Base 3 is also the standard re-entry subperiod after the first transition of the season for subsequent A races. (p. 187)

#### Build 1
- **Purpose:** "Specific training: muscular and/or aerobic capacity; maintaining aerobic endurance, muscular force, speed skills" (Table 7.4, p. 184) `[FLAG: 'muscular' likely should read 'muscular endurance' — see Table 7.4 flag above]`
- **Typical duration:** 4 weeks (under 50) / 3 weeks (over 50). (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Volume target (per Table 7.5, p. 190):** flat across wks 1–3 (no progression — same volume for the three working weeks), recovery wk 4. Examples at 500 h/yr: **wks 1–3 = 12.5 each; wk 4 (recovery) = 7.0.** At 800 h/yr: **20.5 / 20.5 / 20.5 / 11.5.** At 1,200 h/yr: **30.0 / 30.0 / 30.0 / 17.0.**
- **Intensity emphasis:** Advanced abilities (ME and/or AC). (Tables 7.1, 7.4)
- **Key workout types:** ME and AC workouts; AE/MF/SS in maintenance dose. `[SEE: Appendices B/C/D — ME1–4, AC1–3]`
- **Recovery week pattern:** "Last 4–7 days of build 1." (Table 7.4, p. 184)

#### Build 2
- **Purpose:** "Specific training: muscular endurance and/or aerobic capacity; maintaining aerobic endurance, muscular force, speed skills" (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Typical duration:** 4 weeks (under 50) / 3 weeks (over 50, **repeat build 2**). (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Volume target (per Table 7.5, p. 190):** flat across wks 1–3 (slightly lower than Build 1), recovery wk 4. Examples at 500 h/yr: **wks 1–3 = 12.0 each; wk 4 (recovery) = 7.0.** At 800 h/yr: **19.0 / 19.0 / 19.0 / 11.5.** At 1,200 h/yr: **28.5 / 28.5 / 28.5 / 17.0.**
- **Intensity emphasis:** Advanced abilities (ME and/or AC); progressively more race-like. (Tables 7.1, 7.4)
- **Key workout types:** ME and AC; brick workouts most commonly used in build period. `[SEE: Appendices B/C/D/E — ME, AC, brick AE/TB/ME/AC]`
- **Recovery week pattern:** "Last 4–7 days of build 2." (Table 7.4, p. 184)

#### Peak
- **Purpose:** "Tapering for race" / "Specific training: simulating a portion of the race every 72–96 hours; otherwise, recovery workouts" (Tables 7.1, 7.4, pp. 165, 184)
- **Typical duration:** 1–2 weeks. (Tables 7.1, 7.4) "This will give you a three-week taper before your race — two peak weeks and a race week. Some athletes in some situations come into better race-day form with only two weeks of tapering. … if in doubt, make it two peak weeks." (p. 185)
- **Volume target (per Table 7.5, p. 190):** **two-step taper** — Peak wk 1 ≈ Base 2 wk 1 volume; Peak wk 2 ≈ Prep volume. Examples at 500 h/yr: **wk 1 = 10.5; wk 2 = 8.5.** At 800 h/yr: **17.0 / 13.5.** At 1,200 h/yr: **25.0 / 20.0.**
- **Intensity emphasis:** Advanced abilities; race-simulating efforts every 72–96 hours; recovery workouts otherwise. (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Key workout types:** Race-simulation efforts; recovery workouts.
- **Recovery week pattern:** "Recovery not needed." (Table 7.4, p. 184)

#### Race (Race Week)
- **Purpose:** "Removing fatigue / Sharpening fitness" / "Specific training: short intervals at race intensity or greater and decreasing in number daily; rest" (Tables 7.1, 7.4, pp. 165, 184)
- **Typical duration:** 1 week (Table 7.4) / 1–3 weeks (Table 7.1). `[FLAG: Tables 7.1 and 7.4 disagree on Race period duration — Table 7.1 says "1–3 weeks", Table 7.4 says "1 week" for both age groups. The "1–3 weeks" range likely captures cases where consecutive A races stack within a few weeks. (See p. 186: "It's possible that you will have one or two more races in the following one or two weeks. If so, write in 'Race' for each such week so that you have two or three consecutive rows labeled 'Race.' It's rare in triathlon to have multiple A-priority races in consecutive weeks, but it does happen.")]`
- **Volume target (per Table 7.5, p. 190):** flat — Race week (single value, "All") matches the recovery-week volume in earlier subperiods. Examples at 500 h/yr: **7.0.** At 800 h/yr: **11.5.** At 1,200 h/yr: **17.0.**
- **Intensity emphasis:** Advanced abilities; "short intervals at race intensity or greater". (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Key workout types:** Short race-pace intervals decreasing daily.
- **Recovery week pattern:** "Recovery not needed." (Table 7.4, p. 184)

#### Transition
- **Purpose:** "Resting and recovering" / "Rest and active recovery" (Tables 7.1, 7.4, pp. 165, 184)
- **Typical duration:** 1–4 weeks. (Tables 7.1, 7.4) Friel: "this period of reduced training may last a few days or can be as long as four weeks. I've even had athletes take up to six weeks if their season was exceptionally stressful. If this first transition period comes early in your season, which is common, then I'd suggest taking just a few days off from focused training. This may be only three or four days but could be seven days depending on lingering mental and physical fatigue from the race or muscle soreness." (pp. 186–187) "After your last A race of the season, you will probably need a much longer break, more on the order of four weeks." (p. 187) **Long-course-specific note:** "long-course races are much more likely to require a longer transition period than short-course races." (p. 187)
- **Volume target:** **Tables 7.5 and 7.6 do not list a Transition row** — the table goes Prep → Base 1–3 → Build 1–2 → Peak → Race. Transition volume is "exercise" (the athlete's choice), not "training." See Friel's note below; Figure 7.5 shows blank/dash entries for transition weeks. (pp. 187, 190–191, 192)
- **Intensity emphasis:** Basic abilities (Table 7.1, p. 165), "Rest and active recovery" (Table 7.4, p. 184)
- **Key workout types:** "It's all right to 'exercise' in the transition period, but 'training' is forbidden. The purpose is to rest and rejuvenate by not having a workout routine that must be followed." (p. 187) `[SEE: AE1 recovery in Appendices B/C/D]`
- **Recovery week pattern:** "Recovery not needed." (Table 7.4, p. 184)

### Adjustment rules for subsequent A races (verbatim)

> "When scheduling beyond the first A race, you will not repeat the prep period and probably not base 1 or base 2 either. However, you may want to return to base 3 after the first transition period if your basic abilities, especially aerobic endurance, have obviously declined in the last few weeks surrounding the first A race. Repeating base 3 is common and highly recommended when the first A race was a short event and the next is long. You may even want to do base 3 two or more times if there are enough weeks remaining until the second A race. If your basic abilities are lacking, that weakness will greatly detract from training and racing for the rest of the season. But if your basic abilities are still strong after the first A race, then you may want to start back into training with a build 1 or build 2 period. If in doubt, include at least one base 3." (p. 187)

> "A note of caution: Don't shortchange the base abilities in order to do more high-intensity training." (pp. 187, also sidebar p. 187)

> "Subperiod duration actually has more to do with recovery than with age. ## Annual Training Plan construction (six steps, verbatim)

Friel (p. 173):

> "We will follow six steps to produce your ATP:
> 1. Establish your season goals.
> 2. Determine your training objectives.
> 3. Set your annual training volume.
> 4. Prioritize your races.
> 5. Divide the season into periods.
> 6. Estimate your weekly volume."

> "By the end of this chapter, you will have most of your ATP done. All that will remain to complete it is scheduling weekly workouts. We'll do that in Chapter 8." (p. 173)

### Step 1: Establish Season Goals (pp. 173–174)

- **How many goals:** "Set no more than three goals for the season. With more than three, you run the risk of being overwhelmed, and something may be overlooked with all the details. Having too many goals makes it hard to stay focused on what is truly most important. Having fewer than three is also a good option and will give you an even greater focus, so don't feel that you need to have three to succeed." (p. 173)
- **Form:** "State your goals as seasonal outcomes." (p. 174)
- **Measurability:** "Your goals should be measurable. In triathlon, the most common measurement is your race finishing time, so it's the most likely measurement to include when you are defining your goals." (p. 174)
- **Athlete-controlled, not placement-based:** "Goals based on race placement in your age group, such as 'podium at XYZ race,' depend too much on who shows up on race day in your age group. While making the podium is a worthy accomplishment, you have no control over who the other competitors are or how fit they may be. You have control only over your performance. Your goals should reflect that." (p. 174)
- **Where to write:** Top of the ATP. "So now it's time to write your goals at the top of the ATP. Go ahead and pencil them in, and be sure to make them measurable." (p. 174) `[SEE: Chapter 5 for goal-setting depth]`

### Step 2: Determine Training Objectives (pp. 174–175)

- **What:** "There is a space near the top of your ATP for four training objectives, which address your ability limiters." (p. 174)
- **Definition:** "Training objectives are subgoals for your season. If you achieve them, then you can expect to achieve your season goals. Typically, training objectives will be accomplished during workouts. But they may also be achieved in less important races that you do before your season goal events." (p. 174)
- **Purpose:** "to 'fix,' or correct, the goal-specific weaknesses — your limiters — that are preventing you from achieving your season goals." (p. 175)
- **Form:** "A training objective is simply a statement about one of your ability limiters and how you will know when it's corrected." (p. 175)
- **Examples (verbatim, pp. 175–176):**
  - Basic-ability speed-skills (swim): "Improve average pace when doing form 25s at a comfortable effort before starting the build period." `[SEE: Chapter 12]`
  - Basic-ability aerobic-endurance (run): "Increase my run efficiency factor by 10 percent by March 1." `[SEE: Appendix D — efficiency factor in AE2]`
  - Basic-ability muscular-force: "Leg press 2.5 times my body weight by the end of base 1." `[SEE: Chapter 13]`
  - Advanced-ability muscular-endurance: "Swim 1,000 meters in less than 15 minutes by the end of build 2." / "Bike 20 minutes with a normalized power of 250 watts by April 15." / "Run a local 10 km race in less than 42 minutes by 4 weeks prior to my Olympic-distance triathlon."
  - Advanced-ability aerobic-capacity: "Swim 100 meters in less than 80 seconds by March 30." / "Run 1,200 meters in under 4 minutes, 30 seconds by the end of build 1." / "Climb Maniac Hill on my bike in less than 3 minutes by April 15."
- **How many:** "Narrow it down to the four or fewer limiters that are most likely to affect your race performances for your most important races. If you improve these, you are highly likely to achieve your season goals." (p. 176) `[SEE: Chapter 6 for limiter assessment]`

### Step 3: Set Annual Training Volume (pp. 175–178)

- **What goes at top of ATP:** "an estimate of how many cumulative hours you will train during the season, including all your sport-specific workouts, strength training, and cross-training time." (p. 176)
- **Hours OR TSS:** "Athletes who train with the training stress score (TSS) system, as supported by TrainingPeaks.com, should indicate anticipated TSS volume for the year in the annual volume space. The nice thing about TSS is that it includes training intensity along with time. With annual training hours, of course, only training time is considered." (p. 176)
- **Three methods for estimating annual volume:**
  1. **Historical:** "look back at how much total volume you trained in the previous year. … If you successfully managed the amount you did last year, consider increasing the volume 10 percent or so for the new season. This is generally a good idea for athletes in the first five years of triathlon training because they probably still have room for growth. If you are beyond five years in the sport, you may want to keep annual hours the same as last year but do more event-specific training in the new season." (pp. 176–177)
  2. **Weekly × 45:** "determine your typical average training hours or TSS for a week and multiply that by 45. Using 45 as a multiplier allows for weeks or cumulative days when you don't train during the year, as when you are sick or injured, taking recovery days, experiencing business and career interruptions, taking a family vacation, or taking transition period training breaks." (p. 177)
  3. **Race + goal (Table 7.2):** "base annual volume on your longest race and general goal for that event (Table 7.2)." (p. 177)
- **Time-crunched athletes:** "weekly volume is set according to the maximum weekly time usually available to train. … multiply the common weekly hours available to you by 45 and record this in your ATP for your projected annual volume." (p. 177) "TSS is of much greater value than training hours as a marker of annual volume because it also takes intensity into consideration. … Whereas a low-intensity week may produce 45 TSS points per hour, a high-intensity week can result in 55 per hour. A long-term hourly TSS of 50 is a common average." (pp. 177–178)

### Step 4: Prioritize Races (pp. 178–181)

- **Procedure (verbatim):** "In the 'Week' column on your ATP, row 01 is the first week of the new season. Next to that column is the 'Mon.' column. Here, you will write in the date of your season's first Monday. Next, record all the dates for the Mondays of the coming season in the column labeled 'Mon.' For example, the first Monday of October may be the seventh day of that month. So under 'Mon.,' write in 10/7, meaning October 7. The next row down would then be October 14 and is recorded as 10/14. This is done for the rest of the season so that every weekly row starts with Monday's date." (p. 178)
- **Race entry rule:** "After recording all the Mondays for the season, write in the races you intend to do in the coming season in the column labeled 'Races.' Place them on the ATP in the appropriate weeks based on their dates. … For example, let's say that you have an event scheduled for Saturday, May 11, in the coming season. It would be listed in the row that starts with Monday, May 6, and is marked as 5/6 because that row includes all the dates from May 6 through May 12. If you have two races the same weekend, list them both in that row." (pp. 178–179)
- **Priority assignment:** Use Table 7.3 below.
- **A-priority race count rule:** "I'd strongly suggest having no more than three, but, as mentioned earlier, the fewer A races the better. Because you will taper — meaning that you will reduce — your training during a two-to-three-week stretch before the race and then recover for at least a few days during the post-race transition period — again with reduced training — you will lose fitness, especially from your basic abilities. Because you're giving up a bit of fitness to gain race form … there's a downside to these most important races." (p. 180)
- **Pacing of A races:** "Three is about the most that can be managed with time still left to rebuild fitness and come to peak form before the next A-priority race. The more spread out on the calendar the A-priority races are, the better you're likely to do in each of them, as described earlier in this chapter in the section 'Overview of Your Plan.'" (p. 180)

#### Table 7.3 — Race Prioritization (verbatim, p. 179)

| Race Priority | Maximum Number per Season | Race Importance | Special Race-Day Preparation |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | 3 | Most important. Your season's success is determined by these. | Include a 1- to 2-week peak period before race week. |
| B | 5 | Of secondary importance. You want to do well, however. | Reduce training stress for 2 to 3 days before race day. |
| C | Unlimited | Least important. Use as race tune-ups, tests, hard workouts, social events, or for experience. | No special preparation. Treat these as workouts. |

(Source: Table 7.3, p. 179)

#### Two season-design strategies (verbatim, pp. 180–181)

> "With regard to race performance, there are two basic ways to design your season. One is to do no more than three A-priority races, as suggested above, with a taper for two to three weeks before each. This is highly likely to result in your best possible race performances. You can still do several B-priority races, for which you back off training for two to three days prior, as shown in Table 7.3. … The second way is not to do any A races and instead do lots of B races without a true peak for any of them. Your performances aren't likely to be the best possible, but doing this may well make your race season more enjoyable." (pp. 180–181)

#### Single A-priority race scenario (12-month plan)

> "Another possibility is that you may have only one A-priority race scheduled for the entire season. That presents a different challenge. It takes about 6 months to progress through the standard periodization process before the first race of the year. With as long as 12 months until your race, what do you do with the 'extra' 6 months? There are two common solutions. The first, and probably the best, is to designate a second A race that is about 12 to 16 weeks before your most important A race. … The second way to solve this dilemma is to start your base-period training as described in this chapter, but when you complete base 3, do not continue into the build period. Instead, repeat base 3 as many times as is needed to leave you about 12 weeks until your most important A-priority race. Then start the build period of training. You can never have enough base fitness, so this second method will do a lot to prepare you quite well for the specific, race-like training that precedes the 'true' A race." (p. 181)

#### C races as workouts (verbatim, p. 181)

> "Note in Table 7.3 that the C-priority races are treated as workouts, meaning that you rest before them about the same as you would rest before a hard workout. These events are done strictly as 'throwaway' results — meaning that you don't expect to be in top form or even race-ready. They are typically done as tune-ups before A or B races to get back into the mindset and routine of racing if it's been some time since the last one. They may also serve as tests of fitness, hard workouts, or social events done with friends. For the novice triathlete, they are also done for experience."

#### Long-course-specific guidance (verbatim, p. 181)

> "The longer your A-priority races are, the fewer B and C races you should do. Ironman and half-Ironman races require lots of long training sessions, especially on the bike. … For Ironman triathletes, I'd suggest only one B race, preferably a half-Ironman, in the last 12 weeks before your A race. That B race is probably best done around 3 to 7 weeks prior."

### Step 5: Divide Season into Periods (pp. 181–188)

- **Tools:** Table 7.4 (subperiod purposes/durations/recovery) plus Figures 7.2–7.4 (visual layouts).
- **Definition:** "*Periodizing* means assigning training periods to each week of the year in the 'Period' column of your ATP. You'll fill in this column by working backward." (pp. 184–185)

**Backward-fill procedure (verbatim, pp. 185–186):**

> 1. "Start with the first A-priority race of the season. At the intersection of the 'Period' column and the row, or week, of your first A-priority race, write in 'Race' in the 'Period' column."
> 2. "Then go up one more row and write in 'Peak.' Go up another row and again write in 'Peak.' This will give you a three-week taper before your race — two peak weeks and a race week."
> 3. "In the row above your first 'Peak' week, write in 'Build 2.' Now you must decide if you will plan three- or four-week subperiods based on the discussion above about recovery and age. If you are using four-week subperiods, write in 'Build 2' three more times so that you have four weeks of 'Build 2' above your first 'Peak' week. If you are using three-week subperiods, write in 'Build 2' in only three rows."
> 4. "Now do the same thing — three or four weeks each — for build 1, base 3, base 2, and base 1 (see Figure 7.5 for details)." `[FLAG: Figure 7.5 is referenced here but is NOT in the page range pp. 157–188; see Open Questions below.]`
> 5. "In the row above your first week in the season of base 1, write in 'Prep.' This can vary from only one week to as many as four weeks, or perhaps even longer if you have a big gap until it's time to start training for the new season."
> 6. "Now go back down to the row immediately below your first 'Race' week. … Now write in 'Tran' (for *transition*) in the first week after your single A race or the last of your back-to-back A races."

- **Some can use 2-week peak instead of 3:** "Some athletes in some situations come into better race-day form with only two weeks of tapering. Again, this is a part of the individualization you need to consider when designing your plan for the season. There is nothing I can tell you that determines which way to go other than to say it's based entirely on your experience. It's okay to experiment with this to determine which is better for you. But for now, if in doubt, make it two peak weeks." (p. 185)
- **Mixed subperiod durations:** "Some athletes recover quite quickly when focused primarily on the basic abilities, as in the base period, but somewhat more slowly when training the advanced abilities. So these athletes may opt to use three-week subperiods in build and four weeks for each of the base subperiods. Again, this is a personal decision based on experience. If unsure, keep your base and build subperiod durations the same." (pp. 185–186)
- **Friel's preferred prep duration:** "I like to see the seasonal preparation for the first race to be at least six months." (p. 186)
- **Novice rule (verbatim, p. 187):** "The novice athlete — someone in the first year of triathlon training — will focus on the basic abilities for an entire season and will not do any build periods. In this case, base 3 is repeated and replaces all the build subperiods. There is nothing more important for the novice than endurance, skills, and strength."
- **One hard rule:** "The only hard-and-fast rule in periodization that I would strongly suggest you adhere to is this: The closer in time that you get to your A-priority race, the more like the race your training must become. Otherwise, training periodization can be quite flexible." (p. 182)

> "Your weekly volume is not a rigid number that must be accomplished. It's meant only to be a ballpark guide to how much training you do in a given week. We'll get to setting daily durations and making timely decisions about weekly volume in Chapter 8." (p. 189)

## A/B/C race designation

Captured fully in **Step 4: Prioritize Races** above (pp. 178–181) and **Table 7.3** (p. 179). Summary of the rules driving the plan:

- **A races:** maximum 3 per season (Friel's recommendation: "the fewer A races the better"). Each gets a 1- to 2-week peak period plus a race week (so 2–3 weeks of taper). A races are the "anchors" the entire periodization works backward from. (pp. 179–180)
- **B races:** maximum 5 per season. Reduce training stress 2–3 days prior — "mini-taper with a resulting small peak in race form." (pp. 179, 181) For Ironman athletes: only one B race (preferably a half-Ironman), 3–7 weeks before the A race, in the last 12 weeks of training. (p. 181)
- **C races:** unlimited. Treated as workouts — "no special preparation." Used as race tune-ups, tests, hard workouts, social events, or for experience. (pp. 179, 181) Long-course rule: "The longer your A-priority races are, the fewer B and C races you should do." (sidebar, p. 184)

### Linking race priority to phase backward-fill

The race-priority assignments drive the ATP backward-fill (Step 5):
- Each A race week → label "Race" → 2 prior weeks "Peak" → backward-fill Build 2, Build 1, Base 3, Base 2, Base 1, Prep.
- B races sit on the calendar and the athlete reduces training 2–3 days prior; no period-structure change.
- C races → "throwaway"; treated as a hard workout; no period-structure change.
(pp. 179, 185–186)
